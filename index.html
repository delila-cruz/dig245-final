The United States is a diverse country, with a plethora of community types.

This diversity is great, but it also makes it hard for people from different types of communities to relate to each other.

Think about it---what would you imagine a community different from your own to look like? If you live in a city, what would a rural area look like? What about if you live in the suburbs, what could life in the city look like? Do you have any values in common?

Most Americans say that people living in other types of communities don’t understand the types of problems they face. But do we have similar values? Let's see.

*According to a 2018 Pew Research Center Study of 6,251 Americans.

Yes, the things they want are different, but it sure does seem like their reasons are similar.

You might never get the chance to live in a community type that's different from your own, but next time you meet someone who doesn't seem like they understand what you're going through, remember:

Everyone wants safety.


Everyone wants to provide for themselves and their families.


Everyone wants stability.




We all want to thrive.




When I was fourteen, I moved from an urban county in New Jersey to a rural county in North Carolina. To be honest, I was terrified. I had spent my whole life in New Jersey, and I was surrounded by people's uninformed ideas about the South. In my mind, nobody was going to understand me.


To my surprise, though, things weren't like that. It was hard to move for all the normal reasons it's hard for a teenager to move, but I found a great set of friends in North Carolina. I realized that, even though I had different cultural quirks (apparently saying your ma'ams and sirs is a big deal in NC), I had much more in common with my new classmates and new teachers than I thought. Now that I'm older, I'm thankful that I moved. It was good for me to meet new people and have the chance to reevaluate my assumptions.


In today's increasingly polarized and divisive political climate, I hope that American User Stories can remind us that most of our core values are the same, even if we come from different places.
